# Clay Pot Lesson

The clay pot is a humble object that opens many lines of inquiry:
form, function, craft, material, sound, water, pattern, memory, history, design.

This is not a fixed curriculum. Students generate categories and questions, choose media of response, and follow the inquiry where it leads.

## Core openings

- What is this?
- What does it hold?
- Why is it this shape?
- What changes when it is empty, full, tilted, rotated, struck lightly, or broken?
- What kind of memory does a vessel carry?

## Two vessels

Two vessels open comparison naturally:
size, shape, thickness, opening, angle, distance from the ear, rotation, water level, resonance.

Holding a vessel to the ear, moving it away, rotating it, and listening teaches a foundational principle:

**Perception is positional.**

## With water

Water changes tone, resonance, weight, and hand-feel.
The vessel becomes instrument, sensor, and experiment.

## With sticks

A stick can wake the water and the clay.
Two sticks introduce rhythm, contrast, coordination, percussion, and restraint.

Some vessels may chip or break.
Breakage is not failure. It is the next lesson.

## Afterlives

Broken vessels may become:
mosaic, pathway, planter drainage, decoration, markers, sound fragments, or other forms of reuse.

## Response forms

Students may respond through:
play, dance, essay of many voices, exhibit, sound piece, sculpture, story, or forms not yet named.

Openness is not vagueness.
Rigor is not standardization.
