The Four Rites of a DiscoveryPod

Weather • Listening • Carrying • Offering — a spiral, not a ladder.

Overview

What are the Four Rites?

The Four Rites describe how experience moves through a DiscoveryPod. They are not steps to be “completed,” but a spiral the Pod returns to again and again.

  • Rite I — Weather: noticing inner states.
  • Rite II — Listening: sensing the group and the world.
  • Rite III — Carrying: holding care and responsibility together.
  • Rite IV — Offering: giving something back to the circle or the world.

On some days, a Pod may touch all four. On other days, it may rest mostly in one. That is expected and healthy.

1

Rite I — Weather

Weather is the rite of inner noticing. Children learn to sense and express what is happening inside their bodies and minds.

Key supports:

  • Living Weather Deck — cards, colors, sounds, textures.
  • RageDance warmups that map states like heat, tremble, collapse, and expansion.
  • simple check-ins: hand on chest, hand on belly, breath count, posture awareness.

Weather is where words may be optional. Expression can be quiet, symbolic, or fully nonverbal.

Children learn: “My inner weather matters. I can notice it without having to fix it right away.”

2

Rite II — Listening

Listening is the rite of shared attention. The Pod learns to notice one another and the world around them.

Key supports:

  • Elephant Circle & Outlier Gate — shared reflection with two anchoring questions.
  • paired or small-group noticing (“What did you see that I didn’t?”).
  • gentle humor to keep disagreement soft and curious.

Listening includes listening to silence, to animals, to land, to buildings, to “the feeling of the room.”

Children learn: “Many truths can sit beside each other. We can stay in the circle even when we see differently.”

3

Rite III — Carrying

Carrying is the rite of shared responsibility. It does not mean carrying everything; it means carrying something with awareness and support.

Key supports:

  • Medicine Pouch — a physical symbol of care.
  • Turtleback-style roles (first aid beetle, sniffer tracker, grief engineer, story drummer).
  • agreements about when to ask for help and when to rest.

A child who chooses to carry the Pouch has not “graduated” from needing care. They have simply stepped into a new pattern: both held and holding.

Children learn: “I can help keep others safe without having to be perfect or alone.”

4

Rite IV — Offering

Offering is the rite of gift. After noticing weather, listening, and carrying, something returns to the circle or to the wider world.

Offerings might be:

  • a small movement phrase or dance
  • a drawing or symbol placed at the center of the Wheel
  • a sentence or question written on a card
  • a song fragment, rhythm, or soundscape
  • a piece of story from Luna, Maimi, or another character
  • a practical act of care (cleaning, planting, helping someone)

Children learn: “What I carry can become a gift. I have something to offer that is uniquely mine.”

5

How the Rites Interweave

In practice, the Rites rarely appear in a clean sequence. A single moment may include all four:

  • a child feels anxious (Weather)
  • another child notices and sits beside them (Listening)
  • the first aid beetle quietly offers the Pouch (Carrying)
  • later, the anxious child draws a card about the moment and shares it (Offering)

Over days and weeks, the spiral repeats, building a deep sense of belonging and capacity.

6

RageDance Within the Four Rites

RageDance can be mapped directly onto the Four Rites:

  • Weather: feeling heat, collapse, tremble, expansion in the body.
  • Listening: sensing the room, the perimeter, and the pulse of the group.
  • Carrying: holding the perimeter, modeling grounding, pacing the practice.
  • Offering: small dances or grounded gestures shared with the circle afterward.

For some children, movement is the safest doorway into the Rites. Words can come later, or not at all.

7

Design Notes for Facilitators

  • Do not force all four Rites into every session.
  • Notice which Rite the Pod is naturally drawn to on a given day.
  • Return to underused Rites gently over time, not with pressure.
  • Let children name their own experiences of the Rites; adult language is optional.

A simple reflection practice: at the end of a week, invite the Pod to ask, “Which Rite did we visit most? Which one is calling us next?”
