The WeatherWheel

Classrooms have weather. The WeatherWheel is how a Pod notices and names it — together.

Overview

What is the WeatherWheel?

Classrooms have emotional and energetic weather — and it shifts throughout the day. The WeatherWheel is a simple visual way for students and teachers to notice that weather together and name it honestly.

Weather belongs to everyone in the room. Using WeatherDeck cards or other simple markers, a Pod places its collective state on the Wheel and sees the shape of the day. This shared act of noticing can guide simple adjustments: a quiet start when the room needs rest, a movement activity when energy is high.

The Wheel is not a fixed tool. Each Pod creates its own version, adapted to the age, culture, and rhythms of the community it lives in.

This page offers a four-direction starting point. Every community is invited to reshape it.

1

Core Functions of the Wheel

The WeatherWheel can:

  • Expand — when new emotions or sensory states arise.
  • Contract — when the Pod needs focus, calm, or safety.
  • Rotate — when understanding or perspective changes.
  • Center — when someone needs holding or the group needs to settle.

Think of the Wheel as a breathing diagram of the Pod. Its shape can change according to what is needed.

2

The Four Core Directions

Many Wheel traditions use four main directions. DiscoveryPods can use these as a starting point and adapt as appropriate:

East — Beginnings & Light

  • new ideas, first attempts, curiosity
  • the Light suite of the Weather Deck
  • moments of hope, invitation, and gentle offering

South — Heat & Movement

  • energy, activation, play, sometimes conflict
  • Pulse and Scratch suites
  • RageDance warmups, strong emotions, big shifts

West — Depth & Drift

  • grief, reflection, processing, tiredness
  • Drift and Stone suites
  • times when the Pod feels slower or quieter

North — Clarity & Return

  • integration, understanding, resilience
  • moments when learning settles into the body
  • decisions about what to carry forward
3

Placing Weather on the Wheel

The Wheel becomes real when children place their Weather cards on it. This can be done with:

  • a large drawn circle on paper or fabric
  • stones or markers representing the four directions
  • a ground-circle outdoors using sticks, shells, or leaves

Children might place their card:

  • in a direction that matches how their day felt
  • in the center, if they’re unsure or feel “in between”
  • at the edge, if they’re not ready to share but want to be near the map

Over time, the Pod may notice patterns: certain days gather in one direction, while others spread across the Wheel.

4

Rotations, Expansions, and Contractions

Rotation

Sometimes a moment that once belonged to West (grief, heaviness) later feels like East (new beginning). When this happens, the Pod may rotate cards or re-tell the story from a different direction.

Expansion

As new kinds of weather emerge, the Pod may:

  • add new suites (e.g., Seed, Tide, Ember)
  • create diagonal directions between the main ones
  • add layers (inner circle for personal states, outer circle for community states)

Contraction

On days of overwhelm, the Wheel can be simplified: only two directions, or even just center and one side. This helps restore a sense of safety and clarity.

5

Center of the Wheel

The center is where offerings, unresolved questions, and shared mysteries can rest.

  • a question no one can answer yet
  • a feeling that is too mixed to place in one direction
  • a shared object everyone wants to protect
  • a memory the Pod is still processing

The center is not a place of confusion as failure. It is a place of not-yet-knowing that the community agrees to hold together.

6

Connection to the Four Rites

The WeatherWheel is deeply tied to the Four Rites of the Pod:

  • Rite I — Weather: children locate inner states on the Wheel.
  • Rite II — Listening: the Elephant Circle uses the Wheel to talk about shared moments.
  • Rite III — Carrying: DiscoveryPouch carriers learn to watch the Wheel’s balance.
  • Rite IV — Offering: children may place offerings or stories at specific directions or in the center.
7

Adding New Directions

Some Pods may wish to add directions or layers that match their stories and land. Possibilities include:

  • River — for flow, change, and travel between states
  • Shadow — for things that are hard to name
  • Ember — for small, persistent sparks of hope
  • Moss — for slow, quiet healing

Any additions should come from conversation with the community, and where possible, with respect for local traditions and knowledge keepers.

8

What the WeatherWheel Teaches Over Time

With gentle use, the Wheel teaches that:

  • no single feeling has to last forever
  • everyone’s state matters to the whole group
  • there are many ways to move from one state to another
  • some things are ready to be understood now, others later
  • healing is not a straight line, but a circle with many paths

When the Wheel shifts, expands, or is renamed, the Pod may simply say: “Our wheel turned today.”
